What is Quantitative Trading?

Quantitative trading uses mathematical models and computer programs to identify and execute trading opportunities. Unlike discretionary "chart reading," the core belief is: markets contain subtle statistical regularities that can be captured computationally, but are invisible to the naked eye.

Quant trading isn't about "predicting the future" â€” it's about having a statistical edge. As Robert Mercer of Renaissance Technologies noted: Medallion was only right on about 50.75% of its trades. But through high frequency, strict risk management, and position sizing, that 0.75% edge compounded into 66% annualized returns.

Three Major Schools

1. Statistical Arbitrage

Exploiting statistical relationships between assets when they deviate from equilibrium. Classic example: Pairs Trading â€” Coca-Cola and Pepsi's spread oscillates within a range. When it deviates, short the expensive one, long the cheap one.

2. Trend Following

"The trend is your friend." Buy when price breaks above moving averages or makes new highs; sell on the reverse. Most CTA funds use this approach. Simple, but shines during crises â€” many trend followers profited handsomely in 2008.

3. Factor Investing

Building portfolios based on academically-discovered "factors." The classic Fama-French three factors: market risk, size (small-cap premium), value (low-valuation premium). AQR Capital is the poster child.
