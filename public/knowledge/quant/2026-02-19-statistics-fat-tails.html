1. The Beautiful Lie of Normal Distribution

Almost every introductory finance textbook assumes asset returns follow a normal (Gaussian) distribution. Why? Because it makes the math elegant â€” mean and variance fully describe it, VaR has a simple formula, and Black-Scholes rests on this foundation.

But reality is brutal:

  • 1987 Black Monday: Dow dropped 22.6% in one day â€” roughly a 25Ïƒ event under normality, probability ~10^(-135).
  • October 2008: VIX spiked from 20 to 80, multiple "impossible" daily drops.
  • March 2020: S&P 500 crashed 34% in one month with several -7%+ days.

These events are "impossible" under normality, yet they happen repeatedly.

âš ï¸ Taleb's Warning: In the 3rd edition (2025) of "Statistical Consequences of Fat Tails," Taleb emphasizes that under fat-tailed distributions, sample mean and variance converge so slowly that traditional statistical inference essentially breaks down. 1000 days of data can be overturned by a single extreme event on day 1001.

2. Four Moments: Mean, Variance, Skewness, Kurtosis

Skewness â€” asymmetry of the distribution:

  • Skew = 0: symmetric (normal)
  • Skew < 0: left-skewed (negative), longer left tail â†’ crashes more common than rallies
  • Skew > 0: right-skewed (positive), longer right tail

US equity returns are typically negatively skewed â€” "up by the stairs, down by the elevator."

Kurtosis â€” tail thickness:

  • Normal distribution kurtosis = 3 (excess kurtosis = 0)
  • Leptokurtic (peaked, fat-tailed): kurtosis > 3
  • Platykurtic (flat, thin-tailed): kurtosis < 3

Financial data is almost always leptokurtic. S&P 500 daily returns typically show excess kurtosis of 5-20, meaning extreme events occur many times more frequently than Gaussian models predict.

3. Why Fat Tails Exist

  • Volatility Clustering: Large moves follow large moves (GARCH effect).
  • Leverage Effect: Volatility rises on declines â†’ negative skewness.
  • Herding: Panic selling drives correlations to 1.
  • Liquidity Evaporation: Bids disappear in extremes, causing gaps.
  • Nonlinear Feedback: Stop-losses trigger cascading sells.

4. Alternative Distributions

  • Student's t: Most popular; degrees-of-freedom controls tail thickness.
  • Stable (LÃ©vy): Mandelbrot (1963); allows infinite variance.
  • Mixture of Gaussians: Blend 2+ normals to fit fat tails.
  • GEV (Generalized Extreme Value): For extreme value modeling.

5. Practical Consequences

  • VaR underestimates risk by 2-5x under Gaussian assumption
  • Option pricing errors: BS underprices deep OTM options (volatility smile)
  • Backtesting is too optimistic: Gaussian Monte Carlo can't generate real extremes
  • Position sizing too aggressive: Kelly criterion needs fat-tail adjustments
