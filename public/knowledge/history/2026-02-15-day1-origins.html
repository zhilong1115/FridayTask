In China's collective memory, civilization begins with a lineage of semi-divine sage-kings known as the Three Sovereigns and Five Emperors. The "Three Sovereigns" typically refer to Fuxi, Shennong, and the Yellow Emperor (some traditions substitute Suiren), while the "Five Emperors," according to Sima Qian's Records of the Grand Historian (c. 94 BCE), are Huangdi (the Yellow Emperor), Zhuanxu, Di Ku, Yao, and Shun. These are not verified historical individuals but cultural symbols of ancient tribal leadersâ€”repositories of collective memory about human origins.

The Yellow Emperor is revered as the progenitor of the Huaxia (Chinese) people. The Discourses of the States records that both Huangdi and Yandi (the Flame Emperor) were sons of the Shaodian clan. After the Battle of Banquan unified the two brother-tribes, they defeated Chiyou at the Battle of Zhuolu, laying the foundation for the Huaxia people. The modern Chinese term "Descendants of Yan and Huang" originates from this tradition.

Archaeologically, the era of the Three Sovereigns and Five Emperors roughly corresponds to the late Neolithic period (c. 3000â€“2000 BCE). The Yangshao and Longshan cultures, found along the middle and lower Yellow River, align remarkably with the legendary geographical range. While we cannot "prove" the Yellow Emperor was a specific person, the social transitions reflected in these legendsâ€”from hunting to agriculture, from clan to confederationâ€”are echoed in the material record.

The abdication of Yao to Shun is especially compelling. Sima Qian tells us that the aging Emperor Yao bypassed his own son Danzhu in favor of the virtuous Shun, who in turn yielded to Yu, the hero of flood control. This ideal of shanrang (ç¦…è®©, abdication to the worthy) became a central question in Chinese political philosophy: should power be transmitted by merit rather than blood? The question still resonates today.

English

While the ancestors along the Yellow River were building the painted-pottery villages of Yangshao culture, on the other side of the world, the land between the Tigris and Euphrates rivers was giving birth to humanity's earliest urban civilization. "Mesopotamia" comes from Greek, meaning "land between the rivers," roughly corresponding to modern-day Iraq.

Between approximately 4100 and 2900 BCE (the Uruk Period), the Sumerians built humanity's first cities on this alluvial plainâ€”Uruk, Ur, and Eridu. They invented cuneiform, one of the earliest known writing systems, pressing wedge-shaped marks into clay tablets to record trade, law, and myth. The Epic of Gilgamesh was born hereâ€”the oldest surviving work of literature, telling the story of a king of Uruk who sought immortality.

Why did civilization emerge here? The key was irrigation. Unlike the Nile's gentle, predictable floods, the Tigris and Euphrates were violent and erratic. The Sumerians were compelled to cooperate in building canals and levees; this large-scale collaboration gave rise to social stratification, administrative systems, and the city-state. As historians have observed: "Civilization was not born in comfort, but in response to challenge."

Meanwhile, ancient Egyptian civilization was rising along the Nile. Around 3100 BCE, the legendary Narmer unified Upper and Lower Egypt, inaugurating the age of the pharaohs. Unlike Mesopotamia's fragmented city-states, Egypt achieved centralized kingship early. The Nile's annual inundation deposited fertile silt, giving Egyptians a stable agricultural foundation and enabling them to develop their distinctive afterlife beliefs and monumental pyramid architecture.
