On Christmas Day, 800 CE, inside St. Peter's Basilica in Rome, as the Frankish king Charles rose from prayer, Pope Leo III placed a crown upon his head while the assembled Romans acclaimed him "Augustus and Emperor." According to Einhard's Vita Karoli Magni, Charlemagne was "displeased" and claimed he would never have entered the church had he known the Pope's intentions. Whether surprise or theater, this moment symbolically "resurrected" the Western Roman Empire and set the template for medieval church-state relations for centuries to come.

Charlemagne's Empire
During his reign (768â€“814), Charlemagne waged over fifty military campaigns, expanding the Frankish realm into a vast empire spanning modern France, Germany, northern Italy, the Low Countries, and northern Spain. But he was far more than a conqueror. He launched the "Carolingian Renaissance," gathering scholars like Alcuin of York at his court, establishing schools, standardizing handwriting (Carolingian minuscule became the ancestor of modern Latin script), and sponsoring monastic copying of ancient texts â€” an effort that preserved much of classical civilization for posterity.

The Birth of Feudalism
After Charlemagne's death, his empire fractured. His son Louis the Pious struggled to maintain unity, and the Treaty of Verdun (843) divided the realm among three grandsons â€” seeds of what would become France, Germany, and Italy. Imperial collapse, combined with relentless raids by Vikings from the north, Magyars from the east, and Saracens from the south, forced local lords to build their own defenses. Feudalism crystallized: kings granted fiefs (land) to nobles, who swore fealty and military service in return, while peasants became serfs bound to the land. This pyramid of loyalty defined European politics for centuries.

Seeds of the Church-State Struggle
The coronation's most lasting legacy was the precedent that popes crown emperors â€” implying that secular power requires ecclesiastical legitimation. Two centuries later, this seed bore bitter fruit in the Investiture Controversy (1076â€“1122), when popes and emperors clashed over the right to appoint bishops. This struggle profoundly shaped the Western political tradition of separating religious and secular authority â€” a principle that echoes to this day.
