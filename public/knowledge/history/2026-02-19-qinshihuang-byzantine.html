In 221 BCE, Ying Zheng conquered the last rival state Qi, achieving China's first true unification. He coined the title "Shi Huangdi" (First Emperor), signaling a new era. As Sima Qian records in the Shiji, he saw himself as the inaugurator of an eternal imperial lineâ€”ten thousand generations beginning with him.

His most enduring legacy was replacing feudalism with the commandery-county (junxian) system. Chancellor Li Si argued forcefully against reinstating feudal lords: "The empire suffered endless war precisely because of feudal kings. Having just unified, to re-establish kingdoms would be planting the seeds of war again." This centralized bureaucratic model persisted for over two millennia and still echoes in China's modern provincial administration.

Unification went far beyond politics. Qin Shi Huang standardized writing (Small Seal script), weights and measures, currency (banliang coins), and even axle widths. These measures dissolved cultural barriers between the former six kingdoms and enabled efficient governance across a vast empire. He mobilized hundreds of thousands of laborers to connect and extend the Great Wall, dig the Lingqu Canal, and build an imperial highway network.

The cost was staggering. The infamous "Burning of Books and Burying of Scholars" (ç„šä¹¦å‘å„’) saw the destruction of philosophical texts and the execution of over 460 scholars in 212 BCE. Crushing taxation and forced labor made the populace groan under Qin's iron fist. The Han scholar Jia Yi diagnosed the failure brilliantly in his Faults of Qin: "He failed to rule with benevolence, and thus the dynamic between offense and defense shifted." Conquest requires force; governance requires consent. This remains one of history's most powerful lessons.
