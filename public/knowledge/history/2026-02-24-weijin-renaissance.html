In 263 AD, the Sima clan conquered Shu; in 265, Sima Yan usurped the Wei throne and founded the Jin Dynasty. It was an age of extreme political darkness â€” the Sima family had seized power through conspiracy and massacre, yet demanded loyalty under the banner of Confucian orthodoxy. In such times, a group of intellectuals chose a shockingly unconventional way of life.

The Seven Sages of the Bamboo Grove â€” Ruan Ji, Xi Kang, Shan Tao, Liu Ling, Ruan Xian, Xiang Xiu, and Wang Rong â€” gathered regularly in a bamboo grove near Shanyang (modern Huixian, Henan), drinking, singing, and debating metaphysics. This was not madness but the desperate response of a lucid mind confronting an absurd world.

Xi Kang was the fiercest among the Seven. He openly refused to cooperate with the Sima regime, penning his famous "Letter Breaking Relations with Shan Juyuan" to denounce Shan Tao for serving the new dynasty. Sima Zhao finally lost patience and had Xi Kang executed on charges of "unfilial conduct." At its core, Wei-Jin style was the ultimate pursuit of individual freedom under political oppression. When Confucian ideals of "cultivating self, governing family, ordering state, pacifying the world" became hollow instruments of power, Daoist naturalism became the last spiritual refuge.

In 14th-century Italy, the Black Death had just killed a third of Europe's population, and the medieval worldview lay in tatters. It was upon these very ruins that one of history's greatest cultural movements emerged. It was called the Renaissance (Rinascimento), and its cradle was Florence, on the banks of the Arno.

Cosimo's grandson Lorenzo the Magnificent (1449â€“1492) took patronage to its zenith. The intellectual heart of the Renaissance was Humanism. Petrarch (1304â€“1374), called the "Father of Humanism," rediscovered Cicero's letters and called for a return to classical learning. Humanism did not deny God but reaffirmed human worth â€” humans were no longer merely sinners awaiting salvation but beings possessed of reason, creativity, and dignity. From Florence, the Renaissance gradually spread to Venice, Rome, and Milan, then crossed the Alps to transform all of Europe. It was not merely an artistic revival but a cognitive revolution â€” it redefined the relationships between humans and God, individuals and society, past and future, paving the way for the Reformation, the Scientific Revolution, and the Enlightenment.
