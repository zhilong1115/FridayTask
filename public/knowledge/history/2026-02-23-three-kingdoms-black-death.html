English

The late Eastern Han dynasty was a world unraveling. Emperors Huan and Ling had surrendered power to eunuchs and consort clans, and the Yellow Turban Rebellion of 184 CE shattered what remained of imperial unity. Warlords rose across the land, and from decades of chaos emerged one of Chinese history's most dramatic configurations: the Three Kingdoms of Wei, Shu, and Wu.

Cao Cao (155â€“220 CE) was the era's first decisive figure. Controlling the puppet Emperor Xian, he defeated the powerful Yuan Shao at the Battle of Guandu in 200 CE and unified northern China. Chen Shou, author of the Records of the Three Kingdoms, praised him in the "Annals of Emperor Wu": "The Grand Progenitor devised strategies and commanded the realmâ€¦ Truly he may be called an extraordinary man, a hero surpassing his age." Beyond warfare, Cao Cao's agricultural garrison system (tuntian) revived the war-torn northern economy.

The Battle of Red Cliffs (208 CE) was the defining moment of the era. Cao Cao marched south with a force he claimed numbered 800,000, intent on final unification. The allied forces of Sun Quan and Liu Bei met him at Red Cliffs and, using fire ships, destroyed his fleet. The Sanguozhi records in Zhou Yu's biography: "The wind blew fiercely, and the flames spread to the camps on shore. In moments, smoke and fire filled the sky, and countless men and horses perished by burning and drowning." This single battle ensured that China would remain divided into three.

Liu Bei (161â€“223 CE), claiming descent from the Han royal house and guided by his legendary advisor Zhuge Liang, seized the Sichuan basin and proclaimed Shu-Han in 221. Zhuge Liang's memorial Chu Shi Biao and his vow to "bow and exhaust myself, ceasing only in death" became the supreme model of loyal service in Chinese culture. Sun Quan (182â€“252 CE) held the Yangtze region and built Eastern Wu into a maritime and economic power. The three kingdoms coexisted for sixty years until Sima Yan reunified China in 280 CE.

The Three Kingdoms era's legacy extends far beyond politics. Wu's development of the Jiangnan region laid foundations for southern China's later prosperity. Zhuge Liang's rule-of-law governance and Cao Cao's meritocratic talent selection profoundly shaped Chinese political culture for centuries to come.
